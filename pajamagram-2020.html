Pajamagram 2020

Introducing Structured Experimentation During Platform Migration

In 2020, Pajamagram migrated from Magento to Salesforce Commerce Cloud following Magento’s end-of-life. While the migration was primarily technical in nature, it created a rare opportunity to modernize how the organization approached digital commerce.

Magento had supported years of strong performance, but its rigidity limited experimentation and iteration velocity. Functional or template changes required dedicated development cycles, restricting marketing autonomy and slowing the ability to test evolving UX or promotional strategies.

With SFCC, we gained scalable testing capability, deeper analytics visibility, and modular content control. For the first time, the organization had the infrastructure to validate assumptions, measure behavioral impact, and evolve the experience intentionally.

  • Role:
  • Senior UX / Product Designer
  • Scope:
  • Platform migration, structured experimentation, behavioral analysis, UX modernization

Operationalizing Structured Experimentation

Marketing leadership initiated A/B testing with the new platform. Early experiments focused on surface-level changes such as copy adjustments or hiding individual elements.

Given my background in both design and front-end development, I began implementing the majority of experiments directly. Using Monetate’s element-level targeting combined with CSS and HTML modifications, I was able to test structural layout changes, adjust flow hierarchy, and prototype deeper UX variations without requiring full development cycles.

This significantly expanded both the depth and volume of experimentation. We moved beyond cosmetic adjustments to testing meaningful structural changes across navigation clarity, promotional placement, and conversion flows.

As experimentation velocity increased, so did roadmap density. Given our narrow peak-season testing window (November–February), I worked with my manager to:

  • Formalize hypothesis templates
  • Define prioritization criteria
  • Sequence experiments intentionally
  • Avoid simultaneous tests that could contaminate results

We also integrated recurring Hotjar session analysis into our workflow. During peak season, I conducted weekly behavioral reviews and reported findings across merchandising, marketing, management, and leadership teams — providing qualitative context to complement quantitative test results.

Experimentation evolved from ad hoc validation into a structured, repeatable operating model.

Evaluating Legacy Revenue Assumptions: The PZA Flow

One of the most embedded revenue mechanisms on the site was a multi-step personalization and upsell flow (PZA) that had been in place for nearly eight years. It was designed to increase average order value through add-ons and guided customization.

Through recurring Hotjar session analysis during peak season, I observed a consistent behavioral pattern: high-intent users — particularly last-minute holiday shoppers — exhibited hesitation and drop-off within this flow. Many appeared focused on speed and completion rather than personalization depth.

To validate whether the flow was introducing friction during peak traffic, I drafted a hypothesis to temporarily simplify the experience by hiding the PZA entry point and reducing additional decision layers.

After aligning with my manager and securing leadership approval, I implemented and ran the experiment.

Results

  • ~30–35% relative lift in conversion
  • ~20% decline in AOV
  • Net revenue impact positive due to increased conversion

The findings surfaced a critical tradeoff: while the flow increased order value under certain conditions, it introduced measurable friction during high-intent seasonal periods.

Rather than remove the experience entirely, we adopted a contextual strategy — enabling the flow during off-peak periods when engagement supported higher AOV, and minimizing it during peak windows when speed and simplicity drove conversion.

From Experiment to Platform Capability

Beyond validating the hypothesis, the experiment revealed a structural need: contextual control over a high-impact revenue mechanism.

I authored a technical brief outlining the behavioral findings and operational requirement for seasonal flexibility. In collaboration with our development partners, we implemented a native SFCC admin toggle that allowed the flow to be enabled or disabled without requiring code deployment.

This capability remained in place across multiple seasons. We selectively disabled the flow during peak high-intent windows and re-enabled it during off-peak periods. At several intervals, we re-ran the experiment to validate that prior findings remained directionally accurate — reinforcing a culture of conditional decision-making rather than static assumptions.

The experiment evolved from a one-time insight into an institutionalized optimization control.

Broader Organizational Impact

The introduction of structured experimentation, recurring behavioral review, and contextual flow control shifted how digital decisions were made. Rather than layering features or promotional elements by default, the team increasingly evaluated changes through measurable impact and user intent.

Additional outcomes included:

  • Increased experimentation volume and sophistication
  • Greater marketing autonomy within defined guardrails
  • Improved cross-functional visibility through recurring reporting
  • Formalized UX and accessibility standards
  • Clearer communication between design and development

The migration to SFCC became more than a technical transition — it marked the beginning of a more disciplined, feedback-driven approach to digital commerce.

Reflection

This experience reshaped how I approach product design.

Strong commerce performance can mask structural constraints. Meaningful simplification and revenue impact do not emerge from opinion or urgency — they require disciplined experimentation, behavioral insight, and contextual decision-making.

By building the mechanisms for structured validation and converting insights into scalable platform capabilities, we evolved from isolated experiments to a repeatable optimization discipline.
